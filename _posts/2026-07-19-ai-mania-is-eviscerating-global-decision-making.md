I recently read an interesting piece about how the current rush toward AI might be affecting how organizations make decisions. Many companies are now prioritizing AI adoption at the expense of rational planning.

The article describes a trend where organizations feel pressured to invest in AI regardless of whether the technology solves a specific problem. The author points out that many AI projects—particularly internal chatbots—are failing because they rely on poor documentation or lack clear metrics for success. In many cases, leaders are reporting "productivity gains" that do not exist because they are under pressure to show progress. The text highlights that many organizations are struggling with the basics of software management, which makes the novelty of AI even harder to manage.

For AI practitioners and those in the public sector, this highlights a significant risk in governance and public service efficiency. It is easy to purchase licenses for tools, but achieving real results requires a structured approach to data quality and clear project goals. In the public sector, where reliability is essential, adopting AI for the sake of novelty can lead to wasted resources and poor experiences for citizens. For example, a customer-facing bot that fails to resolve a query can damage public trust. It is important to move past the hype and focus on whether a tool provides actual utility.

How can we ensure that AI projects remain grounded in practical needs rather than just following a trend?
